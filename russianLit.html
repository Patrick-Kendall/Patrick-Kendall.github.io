The Idiot

My Take

Fyodor Dostoevsky (1868-1869)

Difficulty: Easy; bizarre style

About the Author

   Dostoevsky triumphs in the portrayal of humans as flawed and proud creatures, with only transient awareness of our lesser qualities. His books contain such crucial lessons in humility that I feel Crime and Punishment should be part of the high-school curriculum.

   Dostoevsky was a Russian man who trained as an engineer and then quickly became a writer. He was arrested by the Russian state for political crimes and subjected to a mock execution, four years of forced labor, and compulsory military service.

Influenced by:

Pushkin, Poe, Cervantes, Hugo, Gogol, Kant, and many others

Influenced:

Nietsche, Einstein, Hemingway, and many others

Briefest Summary of the Work

   Peculiar masterpiece by the man who wrote Crime and Punishment

Brief Summary - INCOMPLETE

   A novel story of an epileptic, Russian prince named Lef Nikolayevich Myshkin who stumbles first through other's romantic pursuits, then pursues his own perplexing romances. The prince is seen as an idealist and is frequently dismissed by others as an idiot. At the same time, every character sees the prince as a force, with strong perceptive abilities, and a persistent good will which they admire and are confused by.

   The prince pursues an unpredictable woman named Nastasia Filippovna. Nastasia and the prince are two of a kind; they see the world as it is. They process the world in different ways; her through suspicion or dismissiveness, him through acceptance or submission. Conversely, she has acts of faith facing certain harm and he reacts with disbelief and resoluteness where the painless course of action is obvious. The inconsistency in the main character's actions is a major source of criticism for the work.

   The ancillary characters often play ironic roles, where their actions conflict with their professed opinions.

My Take - INCOMPLETE

   Most analyses of the book describe the Prince as a manifestation of Christ's love. They focus on the notion that the Prince is meant to be a "completely beautiful human being" as Dostoevsky wrote in a letter. This unfortunately misses the point.

   The primary message is obvious once you know it, and painfully omnipresent throughout. Strangely, I have not found any critique which mentions it. The message has to do with people's motivations and sincerity. From the first interactions between Myshkin and the Epanchins, people assign motives to the Prince. Mr. Epanchin and his footman believe the Prince is a beggar; they are proven wrong. Throughout the book, characters act on whims and act ironically, which critics decry as structural deficiencies in the book. These deficiencies are in fact supporting the primary message: one cannot assume to know another person's motivations.

   This message is felt most by the reader. The structure of the book is bizarre, with several paragraphs and even entire chapters feeling unproductive to the storyline.

   One cannot know another's motivations as that person cannot know them themselves. Motivations, ideas are temporary, subject to whim. This can be seen in every character and best observed in Nastasia.

   The most intriguing moments in the novel are when the Prince goes back on his word or apparently lies. For instance, when he visits Nastasia's home after telling Rogojin he would not do so. Or when he tells Mrs. Epanchin his letter to Aglaya was written "as to a sister."

   One quote from Lebedeff expresses the message:

   "Words and actions, truth and falsehood, are all jumbled up together in me, and yet I am perfectly sincere." (322)

You have confused your motives and ideas (321)

perhaps she had her reasons for doing so (309)

What did you know of my intentions? (280)

War and Peace

My Take

Leo Tolstoy (1865)

Difficulty: Moderate; very long

About the Author

   Tolstoy was born into an aristocratic Russian family and owned serfs (non-racial slaves). He fought in the Crimean War.

   Tolstoy's philosophy is complicated. He was a Christian and a pacifist. He was also anti-church and anti-government, to the point that some refer to him as an anarchist. Tolstoy was excommunicated by the Russian Orthodox church late in his life. Yet, he was an enthusiastic Christian.

Influenced by:

Schopenhauer, Proudhon, Hugo, Thoreau

Influenced:

Gandhi, Lenin, Hemingway

Briefest Summary of the Work

   Philosophical masterpiece; discusses everything from war strategy to religious doubts to a unique portrayal of existentialism.

Brief Summary

   Pierre Bezukhov is the main character. Pierre is the son of a rich count and is well educated abroad. Pierre begins the story as an atheist. He falls into a large amount of money and marries a gorgeous but wicked woman named Hélène. Hélène is rumored to be having an affair and Pierre challenges the man to a duel against his better judgement. He wounds the man and leaves Hélène.

   Around this time Pierre meets with his friend Prince Andrei Nikolayevich Bolkonsky, another major character in the book. Bolkonsky is married to a delightful, small woman named Lisa. He is dissatified with their relationship and ends up leaving her pregnant with his father to join the army. He joins the army in preparation for the 1805 Battle of Austerlitz, in which Napoleon acheives a heroic victory against a larger Austrian and Russian army. A treaty is signed and Austria cedes much of its territory to France.

   Bolkonsky is wounded and captured by the French. He returns home to find his wife dying in childbirth. He then meets with Pierre and discusses freeing the serfs they own. Pierre insists on freeing the serfs as a matter of principle, based on his Freemason beliefs. Bolkonsky is more empathetic, pointing at a serf and stating: "you want to make him what I am, without giving him my means." In the end Pierre does not succeed in freeing his serfs, while Bolkonsky frees a few hundred and provides them a means to sustain themselves. This is characteristic of the two characters.

   The plot continues with Pierre taking Hélène back. Bolkonsky visits St. Petersburg and meets Natasha Rostova. Natasha, her brother Nikolai, and the Rostov family are the other major storyline. The Rostovs are a rich family whose finances are in bad shape due to mismanagement and the son's gambling. Bolkonsky proposes to Natasha, but his father objects and delays the marriage. Natasha then breaks their engagement after being seduced by Hélène's brother. The brother is previously married to a woman he abandoned in Poland. When Natasha learns of this, she attempts suicide.

   A French army marches into Russia and threatens to reach the Bolkonsky estate. Nikolai Rostov rescues Bolkonsky's sister after her father passes away. The book then focuses on Napoleon, his army, and his lieutenants. As the French approach Moscow, Pierre raises a militia and joins the Russian army; Bolkonsky again serves as an officer. The 1812 Battle of Borodino is a climactic moment. The Russian army stands up to the invincible French and both sides receive heavy casualties. The Russians end up withdrawing, giving the French an indecisive victory.

   The Russian army retreats through Moscow to the countryside. The French army follows and occupies Moscow. At some point, Moscow burns to the ground. It is unknown whether the French or Russians started the fires. The Rostov family escape Moscow with carts full of wounded soldiers instead of their belongings. Pierre remains in Moscow and decides to assassinate Napoleon. He instead fights with looting French soldiers and is arrested.

   The French army is forced to retreat and takes Pierre with it. The retreat from Moscow in the middle of winter is a disaster; starvation, disease and desertion annihilate the French army. Pierre befriends a peasant, Platon Karataev, in captivity. Platon is then shot by the French. Pierre's philosophical musings come to a head during the retreat. Pierre is rescued by Russian soldiers led by the man he wounded in his duel.

   Bolkonsky is among the wounded soldiers carted off by the Rostovs. He sees Natasha, forgives her for breaking their engagement, and then dies. Pierre's wife Hélène dies of an overdose. Pierre and Natasha rekindle a romance from earlier and get married.

   In the first of two epilogues, the story is wrapped up, with Nikolai Rostov marrying Bolkonsky's sister and resolving his family's debts. The second epilogue is a philosophical discussion. Tolstoy indicates that all actions are to degrees a combination of free-will and necessity. He challenges the notion that history is written by great individuals; instead suggesting that much of those person's actions are dictated by thousands of small events and necessity. While only a small portion is due to the free will of the individual.

My Take

   This book covers the other war of 1812: Napoleon's invasion of Russia. Interestingly, the book discusses women's rights and the abolition of slavery, called serfdom in Russia.

   The story in this book is excellent. However, the philosophy is what makes it legendary. At its best, the philosophy is discussed casually and makes powerful statements about Russian society and the nature of human relationships. At its worst, the imagery is difficult to understand and is longer than is enjoyable to read. Even so, the book is always well written.

   Pierre's struggles with his faith play a big role in this book. They set the stage for the highly philosophical later chapters. One notable moment comes when Pierre first encounters a Freemason. Pierre suggests his disbelief in God and says his way of looking at the world is opposed to that of the Masons. The Mason responds:

I know your outlook, and the view of life you mention, and which you think is the result of your own mental efforts, is the one held by the majority of people, and is the invariable fruit of pride, indolence, and ignorance. Forgive me, my dear sir, but if I had not known it I should not have addressed you. Your view of life is a regrettable delusion.

   Even aside from the brutal slam at the end, there is a lot to unpack in this quote. The "outlook" referenced is Pierre's atheism. Suggesting it is not the result of his mental efforts and is the outlook held by a majority of people strikes me as insightful. The idea that God exists is alien to our human existence and perhap against logic; so it seems that atheism must be the conclusion many people honestly hold when forced to confront the issue. This takes away the novelty and assumed intellectual superiority one faces when discussing religion with atheists.

   Tolstoy suggests in his other epic, Anna Karenina, that students of what was then called the "natural sciences," which we now simply call science, were unjustly dismissed as nihilists. Tolstoy suggests something similar about followers of religion. Religious people may be unjustly dismissed as unreasonable, when in fact the issue is so obvious, that to assume they have not realized the alien nature of the existence of God is in fact the error. Between atheism, agnostisism and religion, it is a matter of choice, and of little consequence unless one believes in eternal damnation.

   I want to conclude with a quote I feel is revealing of Tolstoy's personality and his writing style:

"To Bolkonsky so many people appeared contemptible and insignificant creatures, and he so longed to find in someone the living ideal of a perfectly rational and virtuous man. Had Speransky sprung from the same class (...) Bolkonsky would soon have discovered his weak, human, unheroic sides"

   This is plainly insulting to the people around Bolkonsky. However, if one considers the reasoning as unconscious, it may be that this behavior is prevalent today.

Anna Karenina

My Take

Leo Tolstoy (1875-1877)

Difficulty: Easy; very long

About the Author

   Tolstoy was born into an aristocratic Russian family and owned serfs. He fought in the Crimean War.

   Tolstoy's philosophy is complicated. He was a Christian and a pacifist. He was also anti-church and anti-government, to the point that some refer to him as an anarchist. Tolstoy was excommunicated by the Russia Orthodox church late in his life. Yet, he was an enthusiastic Christian.

Influenced by:

Schopenhauer, Proudhon, Hugo

Influenced:

Gandhi, Lenin, Hemingway

Briefest Summary of the Work

   Two simultaneous romantic masterpieces with commentary on the good life, and indescribable scenes

Brief Summary

   The novel follows two storylines: one centered around Konstantin Dmitrievich Levin, and a second which switches its focus between Alexei Vronsky and Anna Karenina.

My Take

   This book is phenomenal. It is rightly considered the greatest book of all time. If you do not have the time to read the book, I have heard the movie is also excellent.

   What makes this book, and Tolstoy's work, so special is the precise, psychological description of small characters and then the scenes which play out with those characters. Small characters, such as the portraitist who paints Anna in Italy, are used to send powerful messages about, in that case Vronsky, and also about human nature. This is accomplished in a short amount of writing, then the character disappears.

The House of the Dead

Fyodor Dostoevsky (1860-1862)

Difficulty: Easy; more a collection of short stories than a novel

Briefest Summary of the Work

   Tales and characters from Dostoevsky's time in a prison labor camp

My Take

   The work is eye-opening, gut-wrenching and cruel in its truthfulness. Yet, somehow Dostoevsky retains a light-hearted narration. One emerges from a Dostoevsky work more man than you were going in, regardless of your gender. One also emerges with more empathy, often more empathy for the poor and the sick. One may also gain empathy for the cruel, deranged and the dangerous; as they are often sick or poor as well.
